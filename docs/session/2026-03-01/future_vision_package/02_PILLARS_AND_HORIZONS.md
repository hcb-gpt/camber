# 02 — Pillars and Horizons

This is a high-level blueprint. It is intentionally not a task list.

## The five pillars

### Pillar 1: Evidence-first truth
Camber must separate “grounded evidence” from “proxy signals.”

- Grounded evidence: transcript excerpts, explicit artifacts, stable anchors.
- Proxy signals: affinities, priors, heuristics.

Rule: proxies may propose; evidence must confirm before promotion.

Outcome: fewer confident wrong answers, more honest uncertainty routed to review.

### Pillar 2: Project-centered world model
Everything organizes around the project, not the contact or the message.

- A project is a living object: scope, decisions, vendors, constraints, status.
- Calls and texts are not “logs”; they are evidence that updates project state.

Outcome: the same conversation never has to be rediscovered across tools.

### Pillar 3: Continuity and context
Camber must stitch together what humans already treat as continuous:

- Follow-ups that assume context (“about those windows…”)
- Threads that span days/weeks
- “Same topic, same project” vs “same topic, different project”

Outcome: less re-explaining, better attribution, and fewer dropped commitments.

### Pillar 4: Closed-loop learning with safety rails
Camber improves through feedback, but never at the expense of integrity.

- Human correction is strong learning.
- Model self-learning is weak learning until proven safe.
- Updates are auditable and reversible.
- Circuit breakers exist and can freeze learning without redeploys.

Outcome: improvement without world-model poisoning.

### Pillar 5: Habit surfaces that drive action
A product is what people do every day.

- Morning Manifest becomes the daily habit.
- Redline becomes the correction habit.
- Long Lead Lens becomes the “prevent delays” habit.

Outcome: adoption is not a campaign; it is a workflow replacement.

## The three horizons

### Horizon 1 (0–90 days): Trust and adoption
Focus: make the system honest, measurable, and used.

- Trust recovery: no synthetics leaks; proof gates in place.
- Attribution quality: reduce “unknown or null project” rates materially.
- Habit: one daily surface that actually gets opened and acted on.

What we avoid in H1:
- new “big features” without evidence gates
- speculative automation that writes irreversible state

### Horizon 2 (3–9 months): Proactive project control
Focus: move from “understanding” to “controlling outcomes.”

- Procurement intelligence: long-lead early warnings (category-by-category).
- Scheduling intelligence: commitments, due dates, reminders tied to projects.
- Financial clarity: cost-code mapping and drift visibility.
- Loop closure: unresolved commitments decay into escalations.

### Horizon 3 (9–24 months): Assisted operations, compounding advantage
Focus: shift from “assistant that summarizes” to “assistant that coordinates.”

- Vendor and subcontractor coordination drafts (human-approved).
- Predictive risk: delay forecasts based on decision windows.
- Cross-project intelligence: reuse what was learned on Woodbery for Winship.
- Training flywheel: Redline feedback improves the model continuously.

---

## What we will say “no” to

- Any roadmap that increases complexity without increasing trust.
- Any learning loop that can silently poison the world model.
- Any product surface without a measurable habit and owner.
- Any “math spec” that does not ship with a gate and a test proving the gate.
